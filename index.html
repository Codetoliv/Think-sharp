Proven Research Strategies

Master the frameworks that power expert-level critical thinking

Paul-Elder Framework

Foundation of Critical Thinking

▼

Description

The 8 Elements of Thought (Purpose, Question, Information, Inference, Concept, Assumption, Implication, Point of View) combined with 9 Intellectual Standards (Clarity, Accuracy, Precision, Relevance, Depth, Breadth, Logic, Significance, Fairness).

How to Apply

Question your reasoning using the elements. Ask: What is my purpose? What question am I addressing? What information am I using? What inferences am I making?

Real-World Example

Before accepting a job offer, examine your assumptions about salary being the top priority, consider alternative viewpoints, and evaluate the implications of your decision 5 years from now.

Source: Paul, R. & Elder, L. (2006). Critical Thinking: Tools for Taking Charge of Your Learning and Your Life.

Bloom's Taxonomy (Revised)

Cognitive Skill Hierarchy

▼

Description

Six cognitive levels arranged hierarchically: Remember → Understand → Apply → Analyze → Evaluate → Create. Higher levels require mastery of lower levels.

How to Apply

Use as a self-check ladder. When engaging with information, ask: "Am I just remembering facts, or am I analyzing relationships and evaluating evidence?"

Real-World Example

When reading news: Don't just recall headlines. Analyze the sources cited, evaluate the credibility of claims, and create your own balanced summary.

Source: Anderson, L.W. & Krathwohl, D.R. (2001). A Taxonomy for Learning, Teaching, and Assessing.

Socratic Questioning Method

The Art of Deep Inquiry

▼

Description

Six question categories: Clarification, Probing Assumptions, Evidence, Viewpoints, Implications, Meta-questions.

How to Apply

Use these question stems in any debate, reading, or decision. The goal is not to "win" but to uncover deeper understanding.

Real-World Example

In a meeting where a colleague proposes a strategy, ask: "What evidence supports this approach?" "What assumptions are we making about customer behavior?"

Source: Socrates / Paul & Elder adaptation (2006)

Argument Mapping (Toulmin Model)

Visual Argument Analysis

▼

Description

Six components: Claim (the position), Grounds (the evidence), Warrant (the reasoning), Backing (support for the warrant), Qualifier (limits on the claim), Rebuttal (counter-arguments).

How to Apply

Visually map any argument to spot weaknesses. Draw the claim, connect supporting evidence, identify the unstated assumptions.

Real-World Example

Analyzing a political debate: Map each candidate's claims, their supporting statistics, the logical connections they make, and the qualifications they omit.

Source: Toulmin, S. (1958). The Uses of Argument.

RED Model

Workplace Decision Framework

▼

Description

By Pearson TalentLens — Recognize assumptions, Evaluate arguments, Draw conclusions. A streamlined model used in workplace critical thinking assessment.

How to Apply

Step through RED before making any important decision. First, surface all hidden assumptions. Second, evaluate the strength of supporting arguments.

Real-World Example

Before signing a major contract: Recognize your assumption that the vendor is reliable. Evaluate their track record and references.

Source: Pearson TalentLens Research (2013)

Metacognitive Regulation Strategy

Thinking About Thinking

▼

Description

Three-phase process: Plan (set goals, select strategies), Monitor (track understanding, adjust as needed), Evaluate (assess outcomes, reflect on effectiveness).

How to Apply

Keep a "thinking journal" — write what you assumed, what you discovered, what you'd change. Regular reflection builds awareness.

Real-World Example

After a difficult conversation: Plan what you wanted to achieve. Monitor how your approach worked. Evaluate: What assumptions did I bring?

Source: Flavell, J. (1979). Metacognition and Cognitive Monitoring.
